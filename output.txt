Bingo for Pain
Neck Stiffness
Exercise 1: Pull Your Chin Back
Sit or stand tall and gently pull your chin straight backward, as if making a double chin. Hold for 5 seconds and repeat 10 times.
Exercise 2: Turn Your Head Side to Side
Slowly turn your head to look over one shoulder, then return to the center and repeat on the other side. Perform 10 times on each side without forcing the movement.
Exercise 3: Stretch the Side of Your Neck
Tilt your head toward one shoulder until you feel a gentle stretch on the opposite side of your neck. Hold for 20 seconds and repeat 3 times on each side.
Exercise 4: Relax the Top of Your Shoulder
Sit comfortably and gently tilt your head away from the affected shoulder while keeping the shoulder relaxed. Hold for 20 seconds and repeat 3 times.
Exercise 5: Squeeze Your Shoulder Blades
Sit or stand upright and gently squeeze your shoulder blades together without lifting your shoulders. Hold for 5 seconds and repeat 15 times.
Quick Tips
• Keep your phone or computer screen at eye level.
• Take a stretching break every 30–45 minutes.
• A warm pack may help relieve muscle stiffness.
• Stop if you experience severe pain, dizziness, or numbness.
Dry Eyes
Exercise 1: Blink Slowly and Fully
Sit comfortably and slowly close your eyes, then open them completely. Repeat this 20 times to help spread your natural tears evenly across the eyes and reduce dryness.
Repetitions: 20 blinks
Exercise 2: Rest Your Eyes (20-20-20 Rule)
Every 20 minutes, look away from your screen at something about 20 feet (6 meters) away for at least 20 seconds. This helps relax your eye muscles and reduce strain.
Duration: Every 20 minutes
Exercise 3: Warm Eye Relaxation
Close your eyes and gently place your warm palms over them without applying pressure. Relax and breathe normally while keeping your eyes covered.
Duration: 30–60 seconds × 3
Exercise 4: Look Around Slowly
Without moving your head, slowly move your eyes up, down, left, right, and then in a circular motion. This helps relax the eye muscles after prolonged screen use.
Repetitions: 10 times in each direction
Exercise 5: Focus Near and Far
Hold your thumb about 30 cm (12 inches) in front of you and focus on it for 5 seconds. Then shift your focus to an object farther away for another 5 seconds. Repeat slowly.
Repetitions: 10 times
Simple Pain Relief Tips
✅ Blink more often while using a phone or computer.
✅ Keep your screen slightly below eye level to reduce eye opening and dryness.
✅ Stay well hydrated by drinking enough water throughout the day.
✅ Reduce screen brightness and avoid glare whenever possible.
✅ If you use air conditioning or a fan, avoid direct airflow toward your face.
Safety Advice
These exercises are designed to relieve mild dryness caused by prolonged screen use or visual fatigue.
Stop the exercises and consult your doctor or eye specialist if you experience:
- Severe eye pain
- Sudden loss of vision
- Persistent redness or swelling
- Eye injury
- Thick yellow or green discharge
- Dry eyes that do not improve despite regular breaks and hydration
Remember: These exercises help reduce digital eye strain but are not a substitute for an eye examination if symptoms persist.
3.Wrist Pain
Exercise 1: Gentle Wrist Bends
Rest your forearm on a table with your hand hanging over the edge. Slowly bend your wrist up toward the ceiling, then down toward the floor within a comfortable range. Avoid forcing the movement.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Exercise 2: Turn Your Palm Up and Down
Keep your elbow close to your body and bent at 90 degrees. Slowly turn your palm to face upward, then downward, while keeping your upper arm still.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Exercise 3: Open and Close Your Hand
Open your fingers as wide as you comfortably can, then slowly make a gentle fist without squeezing tightly. Relax and repeat.
Repetitions: 15–20 times
Exercise 4: Stretch the Front and Back of Your Wrist
Extend one arm in front of you. Use the opposite hand to gently pull your fingers backward to stretch the front of your forearm, then gently bend the wrist downward to stretch the back. Stretch only until you feel mild tension, not pain.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3 each direction
Exercise 5: Gentle Grip Strengthening
Hold a soft sponge or stress ball in your hand. Squeeze gently, hold for 3–5 seconds, and slowly release. Do not squeeze hard if it increases your pain.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Simple Pain Relief Tips
✅ Keep your wrist in a straight, relaxed position while typing or using a mouse.
✅ Take a 1–2 minute movement break every 30–45 minutes during repetitive work.
✅ Avoid gripping objects too tightly whenever possible.
✅ Alternate hands for repetitive tasks if appropriate.
✅ If the wrist feels sore after activity, reduce the intensity and allow adequate rest before repeating the task.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended for mild, non-traumatic wrist discomfort and general stiffness. Perform each movement slowly and only within a comfortable range.
Stop exercising and consult a healthcare professional if you experience:
- Sudden severe pain after a fall or injury.
- Increasing swelling or deformity.
- Persistent numbness or tingling.
- Loss of hand strength.
- Pain that continues to worsen despite rest.
Remember: Mild stretching discomfort is acceptable, but sharp pain is not. Progress gradually and focus on smooth, controlled movements rather than force.
Shoulder Ache
Exercise 1: Shoulder Pendulum
Lean forward slightly and support yourself with your unaffected arm on a table or chair. Let the painful arm hang relaxed and gently move it in small circles, then forward and backward. Keep the movement relaxed and avoid using force.
Repetitions: 10 circles in each direction
Exercise 2: Wall Finger Walk
Stand facing a wall and slowly "walk" your fingers up the wall as high as is comfortable. Pause briefly, then slowly walk your fingers back down.
Repetitions: 10 times
Exercise 3: Arm Raises in Front
Stand or sit upright with your thumb pointing upward. Slowly lift your arm forward to shoulder height or as far as comfortable, then lower it slowly.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Exercise 4: Across-the-Body Stretch
Bring one arm across your chest and gently support it with your other hand until you feel a mild stretch at the back of your shoulder. Do not pull forcefully.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3
Exercise 5: Shoulder Blade Squeeze
Sit or stand tall with your shoulders relaxed. Gently pull your shoulder blades backward and slightly downward as if trying to hold a pencil between them. Relax slowly.
Repetitions: 15 times
Simple Pain Relief Tips
✅ Keep your shoulders relaxed instead of shrugging them during work.
✅ Avoid lifting heavy objects above shoulder level if it increases your pain.
✅ Change your sitting position regularly and avoid staying in one posture for long periods.
✅ Sleep on your back or on the unaffected side with a pillow supporting the painful arm.
✅ Use a warm pack for muscle stiffness or follow your healthcare professional's advice if your pain is related to a recent injury.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended for mild shoulder pain or stiffness and should be performed slowly within a comfortable range.
Stop exercising and seek medical advice if you experience:
- Sudden severe pain after a fall or injury.
- Inability to lift your arm.
- Visible swelling or deformity.
- Persistent numbness or weakness in the arm.
- Pain that continues to worsen despite rest.
Remember: Mild stretching discomfort is normal, but sharp or severe pain is not. Consistent, gentle movement is generally more beneficial than forcing the shoulder through painful ranges.
Tension Headache
Exercise 1: Pull Your Chin Back
Sit upright and gently pull your chin straight backward without looking up or down, as if making a double chin. Hold for 5 seconds, then relax.
Repetitions: 10 times
Exercise 2: Stretch the Side of Your Neck
Slowly tilt your head toward one shoulder until you feel a gentle stretch on the opposite side of your neck. Keep your shoulders relaxed and avoid lifting them.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3 each side
Exercise 3: Roll Your Shoulders
Sit or stand comfortably and slowly roll your shoulders backward in large circles, then repeat forward. Perform the movement smoothly without rushing.
Repetitions: 10 circles forward and 10 backward
Exercise 4: Deep Belly Breathing
Place one hand on your stomach and breathe in slowly through your nose so your stomach rises. Exhale gently through your mouth and allow your shoulders to relax.
Repetitions: 10 slow breaths
Exercise 5: Open Your Chest
Stand in a doorway and place your forearms on either side of the door frame. Gently lean your body forward until you feel a comfortable stretch across the front of your chest.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3
Simple Pain Relief Tips
✅ Take regular breaks from your computer or mobile phone every 30–45 minutes.
✅ Drink enough water throughout the day, as dehydration may contribute to headaches.
✅ Keep your shoulders relaxed and avoid hunching while sitting or working.
✅ Practice slow, deep breathing when you feel stressed or tense.
✅ Aim for a regular sleep routine and avoid prolonged screen use before bedtime.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended for mild tension-type headaches related to muscle tightness, posture, or stress. Perform all movements gently and within a comfortable range.
Stop exercising and seek immediate medical attention if you experience:
- A sudden, severe headache unlike any you've had before.
- Headache after a head injury.
- Fever, confusion, or neck stiffness.
- Weakness, numbness, difficulty speaking, or vision changes.
- Headaches that are frequent, worsening, or do not improve with rest or appropriate treatment.
Remember: Tension headaches often improve with gentle movement, posture correction, hydration, stress management, and adequate sleep. If headaches persist or become severe, consult a healthcare professional for a proper evaluation.
Lower Back Pain
Exercise 1: Flatten Your Lower Back
Lie on your back with both knees bent and feet flat on the floor. Gently tighten your stomach muscles and press your lower back into the floor. Hold for 5 seconds, then relax.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Exercise 2: Bring One Knee Towards Your Chest
Lie on your back and slowly bring one knee towards your chest using both hands while keeping the other leg bent. Hold comfortably, then switch to the other side.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3 each leg
Exercise 3: Arch and Round Your Back
Position yourself on your hands and knees. Slowly round your back toward the ceiling, then gently lower your stomach while lifting your head and chest. Move slowly and comfortably.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Exercise 4: Lift Your Hips (Bridge Exercise)
Lie on your back with your knees bent and feet shoulder-width apart. Tighten your stomach and buttock muscles, then slowly lift your hips until your shoulders, hips, and knees form a straight line. Lower back down slowly.
Repetitions: 10–12 times
Exercise 5: Stretch Your Back and Hips
Sit back onto your heels with your arms stretched forward on the floor and your forehead resting comfortably if possible. Relax into the stretch while breathing slowly.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3
Simple Pain Relief Tips
✅ Avoid sitting continuously for more than 30–45 minutes. Stand up, stretch, or take a short walk regularly.
✅ When lifting objects, bend your knees and keep the object close to your body instead of bending from your waist.
✅ Maintain good posture while sitting by keeping your back supported and both feet flat on the floor.
✅ Stay physically active with short walks, as prolonged bed rest may delay recovery in many cases of uncomplicated low back pain.
✅ Apply a warm pack for 10–15 minutes if your back feels stiff, unless your healthcare professional has advised otherwise.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended for mild, non-specific lower back pain and general stiffness. Perform each movement slowly and stay within a comfortable range.
Stop exercising and seek immediate medical attention if you experience:
- Loss of bladder or bowel control.
- Numbness around the groin or inner thighs.
- Severe weakness in one or both legs.
- Back pain following a major fall or accident.
- Fever, unexplained weight loss, or constant night pain associated with your back pain.
- Pain that rapidly worsens or spreads significantly down the leg.
Remember: Mild discomfort while moving can be normal, but sharp or severe pain is not. Gentle, regular movement is generally more beneficial than complete rest. If symptoms persist or worsen, consult a qualified physiotherapist or healthcare professional.
Slouching (Poor Sitting Posture)
Exercise 1: Stand Against a Wall
Stand with your back against a wall so that your head, shoulders, and hips gently touch it. Hold this position while keeping your chin level and shoulders relaxed.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 5
Exercise 2: Pull Your Chin Back
Sit or stand tall and gently pull your chin straight backward as if making a double chin. Keep your eyes looking forward and avoid tilting your head.
Repetitions: 10 times
Exercise 3: Squeeze Your Shoulder Blades
Sit or stand upright and gently pull your shoulder blades backward and slightly downward, as if trying to hold a pencil between them. Relax slowly after each squeeze.
Repetitions: 15 times
Exercise 4: Open Your Chest
Stand in a doorway and place your forearms on either side of the door frame. Gently lean your body forward until you feel a comfortable stretch across the front of your chest.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3
Exercise 5: Stretch Your Upper Back
Sit on a chair with your hands behind your head. Slowly lean your upper back over the backrest of the chair while looking slightly upward, then return to the starting position.
Repetitions: 10 times
Simple Posture Tips
✅ Sit with both feet flat on the floor and keep your hips all the way back in the chair.
✅ Keep your screen at eye level to avoid bending your neck forward.
✅ Stand up, stretch, or walk for 2–3 minutes every 30–45 minutes.
✅ Keep your shoulders relaxed instead of rounded forward.
✅ Strengthen the habit of sitting tall rather than trying to maintain a "perfect posture" all day.
Safety Advice
These exercises are designed to improve posture, reduce muscle stiffness, and promote better spinal alignment. Perform all movements slowly and comfortably without forcing your body into a position.
Stop exercising and consult a healthcare professional if you experience:
- Severe or worsening back or neck pain.
- Numbness or tingling in your arms or legs.
- Loss of balance or dizziness during the exercises.
- Pain following a recent injury or fall.
Remember: Good posture is about changing positions regularly rather than staying perfectly upright all day. Frequent movement combined with these exercises is often more beneficial than remaining in one position for long periods.
Eye Strain
Exercise 1: Follow the 20-20-20 Rule
Every 20 minutes, look away from your screen and focus on an object about 20 feet (6 meters) away for at least 20 seconds. This helps relax your eye muscles and reduces digital eye strain.
Frequency: Every 20 minutes
Exercise 2: Blink Slowly
Close your eyes gently and then open them fully. Repeat slowly to help spread your natural tears over the surface of your eyes and reduce dryness.
Repetitions: 20 blinks
Exercise 3: Focus Near and Far
Hold your thumb about an arm's length away and focus on it for 5 seconds. Then look at an object farther away for another 5 seconds before returning your focus to your thumb.
Repetitions: 10 times
Exercise 4: Move Your Eyes in Different Directions
Keeping your head still, slowly look up, down, left, and right. Then move your eyes in gentle circles in both directions.
Repetitions: 10 times in each direction
Exercise 5: Relax Your Eyes
Rub your palms together until they feel warm. Close your eyes and gently cup your palms over them without pressing on the eyeballs. Relax and breathe slowly.
Hold: 30–60 seconds × 3
Simple Eye Care Tips
✅ Keep your screen about an arm's length away and slightly below eye level.
✅ Adjust your screen brightness to match the room lighting and reduce glare whenever possible.
✅ Blink more often while using digital devices.
✅ Increase the font size instead of leaning closer to the screen.
✅ Take short breaks from screens every hour by standing up and moving around.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended to reduce eye fatigue caused by prolonged screen use and are not a treatment for eye diseases.
Stop the exercises and consult an eye care professional if you experience:
- Sudden loss of vision.
- Severe eye pain.
- Persistent redness or swelling.
- Double vision.
- Flashes of light or new floaters.
- Eye strain that continues despite regular breaks and proper screen habits.
Remember: Digital eye strain is common and often improves with regular breaks, proper screen positioning, good lighting, and adequate blinking. If symptoms persist or affect your daily activities, seek a comprehensive eye examination.

Exercise 1: Open and Close Your Hand
Open your fingers as wide as you comfortably can, then slowly make a gentle fist without squeezing tightly. Relax and repeat to improve finger movement.
Repetitions: 15–20 times
Exercise 2: Touch Each Fingertip
Touch the tip of your thumb to the tip of each finger one at a time, making an "O" shape. Move slowly and try to reach every finger comfortably.
Repetitions: 10 rounds
Exercise 3: Spread Your Fingers Apart
Place your hand flat on a table or hold it in the air. Slowly spread your fingers as far apart as comfortable, then bring them back together.
Repetitions: 15 times
Exercise 4: Finger Walking
Place your hand flat on a table and slowly "walk" each finger forward and backward one at a time, as if your fingers are taking small steps.
Repetitions: 10 times with each hand
Exercise 5: Gentle Grip Strengthening
Squeeze a soft sponge, rolled towel, or stress ball gently. Hold the squeeze for 3–5 seconds and slowly release. Avoid squeezing hard if it causes pain.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Tight Hamstrings
Exercise 1: Stretch the Back of Your Thigh
Lie on your back and lift one leg. Hold the back of your thigh or use a towel around your foot, then gently straighten your knee until you feel a comfortable stretch behind your thigh.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3 each leg
Exercise 2: Seated Hamstring Stretch
Sit on the edge of a chair with one leg stretched out in front of you and your heel resting on the floor. Keeping your back straight, gently lean forward from your hips until you feel a stretch behind your thigh.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3 each leg
Exercise 3: Standing Hamstring Stretch
Place one heel on a low step or stool with your knee slightly bent. Keeping your back straight, slowly lean forward from your hips until you feel a gentle stretch behind your thigh.
Hold: 20–30 seconds × 3 each leg
Exercise 4: Gentle Leg Swings
Stand next to a wall or chair for support. Slowly swing one leg forward and backward in a comfortable range while keeping your body upright.
Repetitions: 15 swings each leg
Exercise 5: Bridge Exercise
Lie on your back with both knees bent and feet flat on the floor. Tighten your stomach and buttock muscles, then slowly lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from your shoulders to your knees. Lower slowly.
Repetitions: 10–15 times
Simple Flexibility Tips
✅ Warm up with a 5–10 minute walk before stretching.
✅ Stretch gently until you feel mild tension, never sharp pain.
✅ Avoid sitting continuously for long periods without standing or walking.
✅ Stretch both legs equally, even if only one feels tight.
✅ Be consistent—small daily stretches are more effective than occasional long sessions.
Safety Advice
These exercises are designed for mild hamstring tightness and reduced flexibility. Perform each movement slowly and breathe normally throughout.
Stop exercising and consult a healthcare professional if you experience:
- Sudden sharp pain or a "popping" sensation in the back of your thigh.
- Significant swelling or bruising after an injury.
- Pain that travels below the knee with numbness or tingling.
- Difficulty walking due to severe pain.
- Tightness that does not improve or worsens over time.
Remember: Flexibility improves gradually. Stretching should feel comfortable and controlled, not painful. Regular gentle stretching combined with daily movement usually provides the best results.
Simple Hand Care Tips
✅ Move your fingers regularly if you work on a computer or use your phone for long periods.
✅ Warm your hands before exercising if they feel stiff, especially in cold weather.
✅ Avoid gripping objects too tightly unless necessary.
✅ Stretch and relax your hands every 30–45 minutes during repetitive activities.
✅ Stay hydrated, as healthy muscles and joints function better with adequate hydration.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended for mild finger stiffness, reduced flexibility, or discomfort related to overuse. Perform all movements slowly and within a comfortable range.
Stop exercising and consult a healthcare professional if you experience:
- A finger that is locked and cannot straighten.
- Significant swelling, redness, or warmth around the joints.
- Severe pain after an injury.
- Persistent numbness or tingling.
- Difficulty using your hand that continues to worsen.
Remember: Gentle movement performed regularly is often more effective than forceful stretching. If stiffness persists for several weeks or interferes with daily activities, seek assessment by a healthcare professional.

Fatigue
Exercise 1: Deep Belly Breathing
Sit comfortably and place one hand on your stomach. Take a slow deep breath through your nose, allowing your stomach to rise, then breathe out gently through your mouth.
Repetitions: 10 slow breaths
Exercise 2: Gentle Walking
Walk at a comfortable pace for a few minutes while maintaining a relaxed posture. Stop if you feel excessively tired or unwell.
Duration: 5–10 minutes
Exercise 3: Ankle Pumps
While sitting or lying down, slowly point your toes away from you and then pull them back toward you. This helps improve circulation, especially after long periods of sitting or resting.
Repetitions: 20 times each foot
Exercise 4: Shoulder Rolls
Sit or stand comfortably and slowly roll your shoulders backward in large circles, then repeat forward. Keep your breathing relaxed throughout the movement.
Repetitions: 10 circles in each direction
Exercise 5: Full Body Stretch
Stand tall and slowly reach both arms overhead as you take a deep breath. Stretch gently through your body, then relax while breathing out.
Hold: 15–20 seconds × 3
Simple Energy Tips
✅ Drink enough water throughout the day to stay hydrated.
✅ Eat regular, balanced meals and avoid skipping breakfast or lunch.
✅ Take short movement breaks instead of sitting or lying down for long periods.
✅ Aim for 7–9 hours of quality sleep each night.
✅ Pace your activities and take regular rest breaks rather than doing everything at once.
Safety Advice
These exercises are intended for general tiredness or low energy and should be performed gently. Listen to your body and stop if you feel worse.
Stop exercising and consult a healthcare professional if you experience:
- Fatigue lasting more than two weeks without improvement.
- Chest pain, severe shortness of breath, or dizziness.
- Unexplained weight loss or persistent fever.
- Extreme weakness that interferes with daily activities.
- Fatigue following a recent illness that continues to worsen.
Remember: Fatigue can have many causes, including poor sleep, stress, illness, nutrition, or medical conditions. Gentle movement often helps improve energy levels, but persistent or unexplained fatigue should be assessed by a healthcare professional.

Exercise 2: Open Book Stretch
Lie on your side with your knees slightly bent and both arms stretched out in front of you. Slowly lift your top arm and rotate it across your body until you feel a gentle stretch in your chest and upper back, then return to the starting position.

Position yourself on your hands and knees. Slowly round your back upward, then gently lower your stomach while lifting your head and chest. Move comfortably without forcing the movement.

Exercise 5: Reach Across Your Body
Stand comfortably with your feet shoulder-width apart. Reach one arm across your body while gently turning your upper body in the same direction. Return slowly and repeat on the other side.
Repetitions: 10 times each side
Simple Mobility Tips
✅ Change your sitting position regularly instead of staying in one posture for long periods.
✅ Turn your whole body when reaching behind you instead of twisting suddenly.
✅ Take short walking and stretching breaks every 30–45 minutes.
✅ Warm up your body with a few minutes of gentle walking before stretching.
✅ Move slowly and comfortably—your flexibility will improve gradually with regular practice.
Safety Advice
These exercises are designed for mild stiffness or reduced movement in the neck, upper back, or trunk. Perform all movements gently and only within a comfortable range.
Stop exercising and consult a healthcare professional if you experience:
- Sudden severe pain while turning.
- Pain spreading into your arms or legs.
- Numbness, tingling, or muscle weakness.
- Dizziness or loss of balance during movement.
- Stiffness that continues to worsen or follows a recent injury.
Remember: Mild stiffness often improves with regular movement and stretching. Avoid forcing your body beyond its comfortable range, as gentle and consistent mobility exercises are generally more effective than aggressive stretching.

3.Thoracic Alignment: The Missing Link to Better Posture & an Ergonomic Desk Setup
Clinical Question

It wants to move.
The healthiest posture isn't the perfect posture.
It's your next posture.
Why It Matters
Your thoracic spine (the middle part of your back) is like the foundation of a building.
If the foundation begins to lean, everything above it is forced to compensate.
Your neck bends forward.
Your shoulders round.
Your lower back arches.
Even your breathing becomes less efficient.
Imagine building the top floor of a house on a tilted foundation.
No matter how beautiful the roof is, cracks will eventually appear.
Your body works the same way.
When the thoracic spine loses mobility and alignment, nearby joints start working overtime—and eventually complain through pain and stiffness.
Current Scientific Evidence (Made Simple)
Research shows that prolonged sitting alone isn't always the problem.
The real issue is prolonged sitting without movement.
Remaining in one position for hours can reduce thoracic mobility, increase muscle fatigue, and place extra stress on the neck and shoulders.
Modern rehabilitation focuses on:
- Frequent movement breaks.
- Improving thoracic mobility.
- Strengthening the muscles that support posture.
- Optimizing workstation ergonomics.
- Building endurance instead of forcing "perfect posture."
Your body is designed for movement—not statues.

Movement is medicine.

Over the past decade, studies have shown that tele-rehabilitation can provide outcomes comparable to in-person rehabilitation for selected conditions, particularly when the program is well designed and patients are appropriately screened.
Examples include:
✅ Chronic neck pain.
✅ Chronic low back pain.
✅ Postural disorders.
✅ Knee osteoarthritis.
✅ Shoulder rehabilitation.
✅ Post-operative exercise progression (selected patients).

✅ Balance and fall prevention programs.
✅ Parkinson's disease exercise programs.

It's improving access, consistency, and continuity of care.
However, some conditions still require hands-on assessment or urgent medical evaluation, especially when there is severe trauma, significant neurological deterioration, suspected fractures, infections, or other red-flag symptoms.
Tele-rehabilitation should complement clinical judgment—not replace it.

It's access.
